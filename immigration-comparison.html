Green Card vs No Green Card

Impact on a child’s education and neurology career path.

Executive Summary

A student's immigration status significantly shapes their educational and professional opportunities in the United States. For a child aspiring to become a neurologist, obtaining a green card removes major barriers related to tuition, financial aid, medical school eligibility, and residency placement. Without a green card, the student is treated as an international applicant, facing higher tuition, limited aid, and restricted access to medical schools and residencies.

Section 1: High School Impact

  • No difference in access to public high schools.
  • Same access to AP courses, honors classes, and extracurriculars.
  • Immigration status does not affect high school academics.

Section 2: College Admissions Comparison

With Green Card

  • Treated as a domestic student.
  • Easier admissions at public and private universities.
  • Eligible for in‑state tuition after establishing residency.
  • Eligible for FAFSA, Pell Grants, federal loans, and work‑study.
  • More scholarships available.

Without Green Card

  • Treated as an international student.
  • Higher competition for limited international seats.
  • Must pay out‑of‑state or international tuition.
  • No federal financial aid.
  • Limited scholarships.

Section 4: Medical School Eligibility

With Green Card

  • Eligible for all 150+ U.S. medical schools.
  • Eligible for federal loans.
  • No visa restrictions.
  • Easier access to research, internships, and clinical programs.

Without Green Card

  • Only ~40 medical schools accept international students.
  • Many require proof of full tuition upfront.
  • No federal loans.
  • Higher GPA/MCAT expectations.
  • Limited seats for international applicants.

Section 5: Residency & Neurology Pathway

With Green Card

  • No visa required for residency.
  • Higher chances of matching into neurology.
  • Eligible for all fellowships.

Without Green Card

  • Must secure J‑1 or H‑1B visa.
  • Some residency programs do not sponsor visas.
  • Additional immigration steps required after residency.

Section 6: Age‑Out Rule (Critical for H‑1B Families)

Children on H‑4 visas lose dependent status at age 21. Without a green card, they must switch to an F‑1 student visa, which:

  • Increases tuition costs.
  • Eliminates eligibility for federal aid.
  • Restricts medical school options.

Securing a green card before age 21 avoids these issues and ensures continuity in education and career planning.

Section 8: Final Recommendations

For families on H‑1B visas, obtaining a green card before the child turns 21 is strongly recommended. It ensures access to affordable education, full financial aid, and a smoother path to medical school and a neurology career. Early planning is essential to avoid age‑out risks and maximize opportunities.
